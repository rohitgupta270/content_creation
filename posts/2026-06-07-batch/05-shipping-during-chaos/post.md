# I Shipped a Product During a Family Crisis. Here's What It Taught Me About Priorities.

Three months into a critical product launch, my family had a health crisis.

Not serious enough to stop work entirely. Serious enough that I was in hospital waiting rooms during sprints. Missing standups. Distracted during calls.

The project had a hard deadline: 90 days. We were 60 days in. I could've:
1. Stepped back (CEO could take over)
2. Delayed the launch (health crisis is legitimate)
3. Kept going, stressed and part-time

I did option 3 for exactly 2 weeks. It was catastrophic.

My judgment was terrible. I delayed decisions trying to preserve mental energy. The team sensed the hesitation. Uncertainty cascaded. We missed a critical integration deadline. I pulled an all-nighter to catch up. Made bad calls in exhaustion.

On week 3, I did something different: **I told the team exactly what was happening.**

Not complaining. Just clear: "My family situation is serious. For the next 4 weeks, I'll be available 70% of normal. Here's my working hours. Here's what I can't do." 

And then I made a hard call: **Reduce scope by 30%.**

Not delay the launch. Cut features. The non-essential stuff — "nice to have" roadmap items — went to version 1.1.

What happened:

- Team knew what to expect (no hidden chaos)
- Scope was tight (what remains is critical)
- My 70% was focused on the 30% of decisions that mattered most
- Everything got faster because it was smaller

We shipped on time. Feature adoption was strong. And I was actually present for my family.

**The insight:** The worst product decisions happen when you try to maintain 100% performance during 70% capacity.

You don't get 70% output. You get weird, half-committed decisions that explode later.

**Here's what I've learned:**

When life hits hard (health, family, personal stuff), the answer isn't to push harder. It's to be honest about capacity and cut ruthlessly.

Most product leaders hide this. They appear fully present while being 50% there mentally. The product suffers in weird ways. The team senses something's off but can't name it.

The best leaders I've worked with are the ones who say: "I'm dealing with something. Here's what changes. Here's what stays the same."

That clarity actually speeds things up.

Pretending you're fine while being a distracted mess? That slows everything down.
