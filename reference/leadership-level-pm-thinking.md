# Leadership-Level PM Thinking (15+ Years)

**The Core Difference:** Junior PMs write tactics. Veteran leaders write judgment.

---

## What Junior PMs Write vs. What 15-Year Leaders Write

### Topic: Prioritization

**Junior PM:**
> "Here's the RICE framework: Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort. Use this to score your features. Problem solved."

**15-Year Leader:**
> "I've used RICE at 5 companies. It works until it doesn't. Specifically: RICE assumes impact is proportional to reach. But that's almost never true. The biggest impact often comes from solving deep problems for small segments. I've watched teams choose high-reach/low-impact features over high-impact/low-reach ones because of RICE. And then wonder why they're losing customers. 
>
> The real decision isn't 'what should I build?' It's 'what are we refusing to become?' RICE is math. Prioritization is identity."

---

### Topic: Discovery

**Junior PM:**
> "Run user interviews. Ask 5 questions. Here's a template. Done."

**15-Year Leader:**
> "I've done 400+ user interviews across companies. And I've been wrong more times than I've been right. Here's what I learned: Most PMs interview to confirm what they already believe, not to discover what's true. You ask leading questions. You interpret answers generously. You miss the actual problem.
>
> The interviews that actually mattered? The ones where the user said something that contradicted my assumption. Those were painful. Because I had to admit I was wrong about something I'd been confident about.
>
> So now I do something different: I interview with a specific goal: prove myself wrong. Not prove myself right. Find the thing I'm wrong about. That's when discovery actually works."

## The Six Dimensions of Leadership-Level Writing

### 1. Pattern Recognition (Not Generic Observation)

**Junior:** "Teams struggle with prioritization"  
**Leader:** "I've seen this exact dynamics at Microsoft, Stripe, and three early-stage startups. High-performing teams do this, struggling teams do that. Here's the pattern."

### 2. Uncomfortable Truth (Not Obvious Advice)

**Junior:** "Communication is important"  
**Leader:** "Most product orgs fail not because of bad processes, but because of bad communication. The uncomfortable truth: you probably know what you need to communicate, but you're avoiding it because it's hard or political."

### 3. When It Works vs. When It Breaks (Not Universal Prescription)

**Junior:** "Use this framework"  
**Leader:** "This approach works at Series A-C when team is <20 PMs. At scale (100+ PMs), it breaks because of coordination complexity. Here's what changes."

### 4. What NOT to Do (Not Just What to Do)

**Junior:** "Here's the best practice"  
**Leader:** "I've made this mistake 3 times. Here's why it failed. Here's what I thought would happen vs. what actually happened. Don't do this."

### 5. Systems Thinking (Not Just Tactics)

**Junior:** "Here's how to run user interviews"  
**Leader:** "How you run discovery cascades. It affects what you build. What you build affects team morale. Team morale affects whether good people stay. This is why discovery matters — it's not about the mechanic, it's about the whole system."

### 6. Specific Examples (Not Generic Stories)

**Junior:** "I worked at a company and learned..."  
**Leader:** "At [Company] in 2019, we had 47 feature requests in backlog. We used RICE and ranked them. Built the top 5. 3 of them flopped. Why? Because RICE doesn't account for execution risk in a 5-person team. We should have built for 3-person team capacity, not the math."

## The Tone of a Peer, Not a Teacher

**Teaching Novices:**
- "Here's the answer"
- "You should do X"
- "Best practice is Y"
- Certain, prescriptive

**Speaking to Peers:**
- "I've found that..."
- "Here's what I'd consider..."
- "It depends on..."
- "I've been wrong about this"
- Humble, contextual, honest

## Examples: Leadership-Level Posts

### Example 1: On Payments & Risk

**Title Concept:** "Everyone talks about embedded payments. Nobody talks about what breaks when you go from marketplace to bank."

**Structure:**
1. **Pattern:** "I've worked on payments at three companies. Every time we tried to move upmarket in payments, we hit the same wall."
2. **Uncomfortable Truth:** "It's not technology. It's regulatory and compliance. And it's far more complex than the startup world admits."
3. **When It Works/When It Breaks:** "Direct-to-consumer? Embedded payments works. Handling merchants? You need a different model. Handling B2B? Different again."
4. **What NOT to Do:** "Don't assume payments infrastructure is portable across segments. I've watched two teams burn 18 months on this assumption."
5. **Systems Thinking:** "Payment decisions cascade to ops, compliance, pricing. You can't treat payments as a feature."
6. **Specific Example:** "At [Company], we had 47% merchant churn because our payment experience was built for consumers, not businesses. Cost us $2M in ARR before we realized."

---

### Example 2: On Product Leadership & Stakeholders

**Title Concept:** "The hardest decision isn't what to build. It's telling the CEO no."

**Structure:**
1. **Pattern:** "I've reported to 4 CEOs. Every single one had a feature they believed in that didn't align with data."
2. **Uncomfortable Truth:** "It's not about the data. It's about managing up, political capital, and sometimes just accepting that you can't win every battle."
3. **When It Works/When It Breaks:** "Early stage? You might have to build what the CEO wants. Post-PMF? You can push back harder because you have data. But at some companies, politics always wins."
4. **What NOT to Do:** "Don't think data alone will convince a confident executive. It won't. You need trust, political capital, and an alternative they can live with."
5. **Systems Thinking:** "If you always say yes, you lose credibility. If you always say no, you get fired. Leadership is balance."
6. **Specific Example:** "CEO wanted to pivot to mobile-first. All data said web-first was right. Took 4 months to build trust enough to push back. Then we tested mobile-first with 10% users, data proved my point, but it still took another month to align. That's 5 months of political work for one decision."

## Writing Checklist for Leadership-Level Posts

Before publishing:

- [ ] **Pattern Recognition** — Does this show I've seen this across multiple companies/situations?
- [ ] **Uncomfortable Truth** — What do most people get wrong about this?
- [ ] **Context-Dependency** — When does this work vs. break?
- [ ] **Failure Lesson** — What have I (or others) done wrong here?
- [ ] **Systems Thinking** — How does this cascade through the org/product?
- [ ] **Specific Examples** — Real numbers, real situations from my experience?
- [ ] **Peer Tone** — Am I speaking to other experienced PMs, not teaching juniors?
- [ ] **Uncomfortable Honesty** — Am I being honest about complexity, tradeoffs, and uncertainty?
- [ ] **15-Year Signal** — Would someone with 5 years think "I hadn't considered that" or "that's obvious"?

**If you can't answer "yes" to most of these, the post isn't leadership-level yet.**

## The Payoff

When you write at this level, you signal:
- Deep experience (not theoretical knowledge)
- Honest assessment of complexity (not simplistic solutions)
- Judgment built on pattern recognition (not frameworks)
- Humility about uncertainty (not false confidence)
- Peer-level conversation (not teacher/student dynamic)

This attracts:
- Other experienced PMs (your true peer audience)
- Founders and leaders who respect depth
- Recruiters looking for serious talent
- People who want to learn judgment, not tactics

It repels:
- Junior PMs (they want simple answers)
- People seeking best practices (they want frameworks)
- People who want quick tips (this is long-form)

That's exactly what you want.
