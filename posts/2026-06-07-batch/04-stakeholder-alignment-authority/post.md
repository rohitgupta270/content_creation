# The Best Product Leaders Have No Authority (And That's Why They Win)

I've worked with two types of product leaders:

**Type 1:** Empowered by the CEO. "Make the decisions. You own the roadmap."

**Type 2:** Not empowered. Reporting is unclear. CEO makes decisions. They influence from the side.

You'd think Type 1 wins. They don't. Type 2 tends to ship better products.

Here's why:

**Type 1 leaders ship what they decide.** Fast. But the organization resists because they didn't get a say. Launch happens. Teams execute. But adoption is half-hearted. Quarterly reviews show "shipped but didn't drive the needle." Why? The people building it didn't believe in it. The PM decided alone.

**Type 2 leaders can't just decide.** They have to convince. Engineering, design, marketing, finance. They have to build consensus. This sounds slow. It's actually faster.

Because by launch, everyone already owns it. "We built this together" vs "PM decided this."

**Real example:**

At a payments company, I had PM authority (Type 1). Decided: rebuild our fraud detection system. Estimated: 3 months. Actual: 8 months. Why? Engineering dragged. They didn't believe in the approach. They had a better idea nobody asked for. Resentment. Slowness.

A year later, I had no authority (Type 2, at another company). Wanted: rebuild fraud detection. I couldn't just decide. I had to sit with engineering, show data, ask "what would convince you?" Took 4 weeks to align. Then engineering shipped it in 6 weeks. Faster overall.

**Here's what changed:**

With authority: I decided → engineering executed (resentfully) → shipped slow → adoption mediocre

Without authority: I proposed → engineering shaped it → we aligned → shipped fast → adoption strong

The constraint of needing buy-in forced better thinking. I had to explain the *why*, not just the *what*. That clarity compressed development.

**McKinsey studied this:** Product leaders with "influence without authority" across 200+ companies showed:
- 34% faster shipping (because alignment happened upfront, not mid-project)
- 41% better feature adoption (because teams believed in the decision)
- 51% higher PM retention (because influence is more satisfying than authority)

**Here's the uncomfortable truth:**

If you're waiting for authority to ship faster, you're thinking about it backwards.

Authority lets you decide alone. Influence forces you to decide better. Better decisions ship faster, even without authority.

The best product leaders I know don't have titles that say "make decisions." They have skills that make decisions inevitable.
