# Writing Rules for Rohit's Posts

**These rules apply to ALL posts. No exceptions. Check every post against this list before publishing.**

---

## 1. No Em Dashes — Ever

**Rule:** Em dashes (—) are not allowed. Replace with:
- Comma
- Colon
- Restructure the sentence

**Examples:**

❌ "Here's what I've learned — patterns matter."  
✅ "Here's what I've learned: patterns matter."

❌ "At one company, we tried this approach — it failed."  
✅ "At one company, we tried this approach. It failed."

❌ "The PMs winning right now — they think differently."  
✅ "The PMs winning right now think differently."

---

## 2. Cut Filler Openers

**Rule:** Remove these warm-ups entirely:
- "I'm excited to..."
- "First of all..."
- "To be honest..."
- "Let me be clear..."
- "As I mentioned..."
- "So here's the thing..."

**Examples:**

❌ "I'm excited to share what I learned about payments."  
✅ "Here's what I learned about payments."

❌ "First of all, you need to understand the problem."  
✅ "You need to understand the problem."

❌ "To be honest, most teams get this wrong."  
✅ "Most teams get this wrong."

---

## 3. Active Voice — Always

**Rule:** The subject performs the action, not receives it.

**Examples:**

❌ "The fraud detection can be loose because fraud is rare."  
✅ "You can loosen fraud detection because fraud is rare."

❌ "This shift was realized when a merchant asked me..."  
✅ "I realized this when a merchant asked me..."

❌ "The system was built for a 5-year cycle."  
✅ "We built the system for a 5-year cycle."

❌ "The post is being published tomorrow."  
✅ "I'm publishing the post tomorrow."

---

## 4. No Hedging Language

**Rule:** Remove these words/phrases entirely:
- "I think..."
- "I believe..."
- "I would argue..."
- "In my opinion..."
- "We hope to..."
- "Arguably..."
- "Might..."
- "Perhaps..."
- "It seems like..."

**Examples:**

❌ "I think most teams get this wrong."  
✅ "Most teams get this wrong."

❌ "I believe the cycle will turn in 2 years."  
✅ "The cycle will turn in 2 years."

❌ "We hope to make this work."  
✅ "We're making this work."

❌ "This might be the biggest issue."  
✅ "This is the biggest issue."

---

## 5. Don't Shorten — Improve Flow

**Rule:** More words are fine if they improve clarity and sound. Less words that are unclear is bad.

**Focus on flow, not brevity.**

**Examples:**

❌ "Fraud 10x'd. System broke." (Too choppy)  
✅ "Fraud spiked 10x. The system broke because we weren't ready."

❌ "Defaults low. Cut corners. Cycle turns. System fails." (Confusing)  
✅ "Defaults are low, so teams cut corners on risk. When the cycle turns, the system fails."

❌ "WalletFirst. CardLast." (Awkward abbreviation)  
✅ "We shifted to wallet-first checkout. Card entry is still an option."

---

## 6. Sound Natural Spoken Aloud

**Rule:** If you wouldn't say it in a conversation with a peer, don't write it.

**Read it out loud. If it sounds weird, fix it.**

**Examples:**

❌ "The normalization of digital payment mechanisms necessitates architectural reconsideration."  
✅ "Digital wallets are now the default. Your payment architecture needs to change."

❌ "Leverage the temporal advantages of instantaneous settlement infrastructure."  
✅ "Use real-time payments to manage your cash flow better."

❌ "The pedagogical implications of Gen Z behavioral patterns..."  
✅ "Gen Z pays differently because they grew up with wallets instead of cards."

---

## 7. Parallel Structure in Lists

**Rule:** When listing items, use the same grammatical structure for each.

**Examples:**

❌ 
- "Building better products"
- "Teams that prioritize"
- "Understanding your users"

✅ 
- "Build better products"
- "Prioritize your team"
- "Understand your users"

❌ 
- "Fraud detection can be loose"
- "Risk algorithms should be simple"
- "Optimize for speed"

✅ 
- "Loosen fraud detection"
- "Simplify risk algorithms"
- "Optimize for speed"

---

## 8. Remove Redundant Qualifiers

**Rule:** Don't say the same thing twice with different words.

**Examples:**

❌ "roughly ~$9.7M" → ✅ "~$9.7M"

❌ "There are two possible approaches: first, X. Or second, Y." → ✅ "Two approaches: X or Y."

❌ "In my experience and from what I've seen..." → ✅ "I've seen..."

❌ "The key insight, which is important to understand..." → ✅ "The key insight..."

❌ "Each and every team" → ✅ "Every team"

---

## 9. Word Choice That Protects Narrative Framing

**Rule:** Word choice shapes how readers interpret your point. Choose words that reinforce your narrative.

**Examples:**

If you want to position something as inevitable:
❌ "Might happen in a few years"  
✅ "Will happen by 2027"

If you want to position something as urgent:
❌ "Teams should consider this"  
✅ "Teams need to move now"

If you want to position yourself as experienced:
❌ "I learned one time when..."  
✅ "I've seen this pattern at three companies"

If you want to position something as non-negotiable:
❌ "It's probably important to..."  
✅ "You have to..."

---

## 10. Real Examples & Concrete Data (MANDATORY)

**Rule:** Every post MUST include specific company names, numbers, and market data. No generic examples.

**What MUST be present:**
- Real company/product names: "Wise", "Stripe", "Booking.com" — NOT "a payments company"
- Specific metrics: "31% conversion lift", "45% YoY growth", "$11B valuation" — NOT "improved significantly"
- Market context: "B2B SMBs in Europe", "67% of travelers", "emerging markets" — NOT "many companies"
- Real case studies: Both wins and failures from named companies
- Data attribution: "According to McKinsey 2024..." or "Hopper's Q3 report showed..."

**Examples:**

❌ "A travel startup pivoted and saw better results."  
✅ "Hopper shifted from 'all options' to 'one recommendation' → 31% conversion lift. Skyscanner stayed with option explosion → 4% decline."

❌ "Real-time payments help merchants."  
✅ "Wise repositioned real-time payments from 'instant transfers' to 'working capital optimization' → 45% YoY B2B growth."

❌ "Most product teams get this wrong."  
✅ "78% of companies adding AI bolt it onto existing processes. 22% rethink the process first. The second group ships 2.3x faster (per McKinsey study)."

**For personal examples** (from Rohit's experience):
- Keep company names vague if confidential: "a payments company I advised"
- But ground it with real market data to support your point
- Example: "We spent $200K building X. Meanwhile, Stripe shipped it 6 months in (vendor velocity risk: 45% probability)."
