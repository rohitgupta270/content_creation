# Real Examples & Concrete Data — MANDATORY Standard

**Every post MUST include real company names, specific numbers, and market data.** This is non-negotiable. Generic examples = forgettable posts.

---

## What Must Be Present in Every Post

### 1. **Real Company/Product Names**

NOT: "a payments company", "a travel startup", "a product leader"  
YES: "Wise", "Stripe", "Booking.com", "Hopper", "Square", "Figma", "Loom"

**Example:**
- ❌ "A payments company pivoted their messaging."
- ✅ "Wise shifted from 'instant transfers' to 'working capital optimization.'"

### 2. **Specific Metrics (Numbers)**

NOT: "significant growth", "much better", "improved dramatically"  
YES: "31% conversion lift", "45% YoY growth", "$11B valuation", "2.3x faster"

**Example:**
- ❌ "The conversion rate went up significantly."
- ✅ "Conversion rate increased 31%. AOV increased $34."

### 3. **Market Context & Segments**

NOT: "most companies", "in the industry"  
YES: "B2B SMBs in Europe", "67% of travelers", "78% of companies", "emerging markets"

**Example:**
- ❌ "Companies are adopting this approach."
- ✅ "22% of companies adopting AI are rethinking their process. 78% bolt it onto existing processes."

### 4. **Real Case Studies (Both Wins & Losses)**

Always show winners AND losers to make your point credible.

**Example:**
- ✅ "Hopper: 31% conversion lift. TripAdvisor Experiences: 28% AOV increase. Skyscanner (still using option explosion): 4% decline."

### 5. **Data Attribution**

When citing stats, always attribute the source.

NOT: "Studies show that..."  
YES: "According to McKinsey's 2024 payments survey...", "Phocuswire's travel AI report shows...", "Hopper's Q3 earnings reported..."

**Example:**
- ❌ "Studies show most travelers want fewer options."
- ✅ "According to Phocuswire's 2024 travel AI report, 73% of travelers say too many options makes booking harder."

## For Personal Examples (From Rohit's Experience)

When sharing personal examples that are confidential, keep company names vague but ground them with real market data.

**Format:**
1. State the personal example vaguely: "a company I led", "a payments startup I advised"
2. Add specific numbers from that experience: "$200K spent", "18 months timeline", "6 months competitor advantage"
3. Anchor with real market data to prove the lesson: "Stripe ships faster than 99% of SaaS companies (180 engineers on platform)"

**Example:**
```
❌ "We made a bad decision once. We should have researched more."

✅ "We spent $200K building infrastructure. Six months in, Stripe shipped it as a feature. 
The lesson: I hadn't accounted for vendor velocity. Stripe's platform team (180 engineers) 
ships faster than 99% of SaaS companies. The probability they'd ship our exact need 
before we finished wasn't 10%. It was 45%."
```

## Examples: Bad → Good

### Example 1: Payments Post

**BAD (Generic):**
"Real-time payments help businesses manage cash flow better. A payments company I know shifted their positioning and saw great results."

**GOOD (Specific):**
"Wise repositioned real-time payments from 'instant transfers' (speed narrative) to 'working capital optimization for SMBs' (outcome narrative). Result: B2B revenue grew 45% YoY, now 30% of total revenue, dominating in Europe and Southeast Asia."

---

### Example 2: Travel Post

**BAD (Generic):**
"Travel platforms should reduce options instead of showing more. One travel app tested this and got better conversion."

**GOOD (Specific):**
"Hopper: shifted from 'show all options' to 'one strong recommendation' → 31% conversion lift. TripAdvisor Experiences: reduced default options 50+ → 8 → 28% AOV increase. Meanwhile Skyscanner (still showing 200+ options): booking conversion declined 4% YoY. The data is clear: personalized curation beats exhaustive search."

---

### Example 3: AI/Product Post

**BAD (Generic):**
"Most product teams aren't ready for AI. The teams that rethink their processes see better results."

**GOOD (Specific):**
"78% of companies adding AI bolt it onto existing processes. 22% rethink the process first. McKinsey's 2023-2024 study found the second group ships 2.3x faster with identical hit rates. Specific examples: Loom shifted to monthly planning (vs. quarterly) using AI → doubled release frequency. Figma integrated AI for continuous user research → 24% feature relevance improvement."

---

### Example 4: Product Leadership Post

**BAD (Generic):**
"Product leaders should think about identity. Companies with identity clarity move faster."

**GOOD (Specific):**
"Wise (2012): 'We are B2B infrastructure, never consumer.' That identity decision prevented 100 feature debates. Now $11B valuation with 97% institutional revenue. Stripe (2010): 'We are software for payments, not a bank.' Kept them focused. Avoided 4 pivots competitors attempted (Square tried payments + wallets + SMB tools). Shopify (2006): 'We are merchant OS, not marketplace/fulfillment.' 15 years later, that clarity compounds. Teams with written, enforced identity ship decisions 6-8 weeks faster than teams still debating it."

## Checklist Before Publishing Any Post

- [ ] Does the post name specific companies? (Wise, Stripe, Hopper, etc.) — NOT vague descriptions
- [ ] Does it include specific numbers/percentages? (31%, 45% growth, $11B, etc.) — NOT "improved"
- [ ] Is market context clear? (Which segment? Which region? What % of market?) — NOT "in the industry"
- [ ] Are sources attributed if citing research? ("McKinsey 2024 survey...", not "studies show...")
- [ ] Are there both win and loss examples? (What worked AND what didn't)
- [ ] For personal examples: is it grounded with real market data? (Not just "I learned...")

**If ANY of these are missing, the post is not ready.**

---

## Why This Matters

- **Specificity = credibility.** "Wise repositioned to working capital" is 10x more credible than "a payments company pivoted."
- **Numbers = memorable.** "31% conversion lift" sticks in readers' minds. "Better results" is forgotten in seconds.
- **Case studies = proof.** Showing real companies (wins AND losses) proves you understand the pattern, not just stating opinion.
- **Attribution = authority.** Citing McKinsey or Phocuswire signals you're reading the right sources and thinking deeply.

This is what separates thought leadership from generic PM advice.
