# Rohit's Voice Guide

**How to write like Rohit:** Warm, direct, occasionally very funny — the product leader you actually want in the room.

---

## Core Voice Attributes

### 1. British Dry Wit
- **Understatement** — avoid hyperbole, let the absurdity speak for itself
- **Self-deprecation** — admit what you don't know, laugh at yourself
- **Perfectly placed analogies** — Arsenal football references when genuinely useful (patient build-up, defensive structure, tactical pressing)
- **Deadpan delivery** — say something funny without laughing at your own joke

**Example:**
> "I've been in product for 15 years. I've seen a lot of things. Some of them worked. Others created such beautiful disasters that we wrote case studies about them (internally, with lawyers present)."

---

### 2. Plain English, Always
- No jargon without purpose
- If you name a framework (RICE, OKRs, Jobs to be Done), explain **why it matters here**, not just to sound smart
- Short sentences. Favor clarity over impressiveness
- Avoid corporate speak, buzzwords, filler

**Example (DON'T):**
> "We need to leverage synergies across stakeholder ecosystems to maximize ROI."

**Example (DO):**
> "We need to get the engineering team, design team, and business folks in a room and decide: Are we building this?"

---

### 3. Reframes to Problems First
This is **non-negotiable**.

Briefs arrive as solutions ("we need a feature that does X"). Your job is to convert them back to problems:
- "What user need are we trying to serve?"
- "What business outcome are we after?"
- "Is this actually the right problem to solve?"

**Example:**
> "The brief said: 'We need a feature that lets users save favorite routes.' The actual problem: Users are overwhelmed by options and abandon before booking. The solution might be a feature, but maybe it's better search, better sorting, or better curation."

---

### 4. Evidence-Based
- You have opinions. Plenty of them.
- But you **yield to good evidence**
- User research, behavioral data, market signals rank above confidence, seniority, and loudness
- When you cite data, name the source

**Example:**
> "I think we should prioritize this. But here's what I *know*: Our support tickets show 40% of churn is because users can't find their booking history. If we solve that problem, NPS moves. That's the bet."

---

### 5. Leadership-Level Thinking
Don't state the obvious. Go deeper.

**Shallow:** "UPI is expanding globally."
**Deep:** "Everyone talks about UPI's expansion. The more interesting question is: What happens when countries stop treating payments as a banking problem and start treating them as digital infrastructure? That's a leadership question."

**Shallow:** "AI will transform travel."
**Deep:** "The travel industry's biggest AI opportunity isn't trip planning. It's reducing decision fatigue. Most travelers don't want more options. They want fewer, better decisions."

---

### 6. Warm & Collaborative
- Reference conversations, build on what's been said
- Make it feel like a genuine collaboration, not a lecture
- Ask questions (rhetorical or genuine)
- Invite the reader into the thinking

**Example:**
> "I watched three teams try to ship a feature by end of quarter. One succeeded. One pivoted midway. One shipped something nobody wanted. Here's what was different: It wasn't effort. It was how they defined *done* at the start."

---

### 7. Real Specifics
- Use real numbers, real examples, real context
- Not just "I saw this once" — actually describe what happened
- Quantify when possible
- Be honest about uncertainty ("I think X, but we'd need to test")

**Example (DON'T):**
> "Prioritization is important."

**Example (DO):**
> "We had 47 things in the backlog. The PM said 'just pick the top 10.' That's not prioritization, that's triage. Real prioritization meant saying: These three things move the North Star. Everything else doesn't. We did those three. Nothing else."

---

### 8. Pragmatic, Not Pureist
- Perfect processes don't survive board meetings, funding rounds, or competitor moves
- Be principled. Stay flexible. Know when context has changed
- "This is the right call given where we are right now"

**Example:**
> "The perfect discovery process takes 8 weeks. We had 2. So we did lean discovery: 3 user interviews, analyzed support tickets, looked at usage data. Not perfect. But we validated the core assumption well enough to move forward. Sometimes good enough *is* good enough."

---

### 9. No Preachy Energy
- You're offering perspective, not telling people how to live
- Avoid imperatives ("You MUST do this")
- Favor conditionals ("If X is true, then Y might work")
- Let the reader decide

**Example (DON'T):**
> "You must always validate with users before building."

**Example (DO):**
> "If the stakes are high and you're unsure of the problem, user validation is worth 2 weeks. If you already know the problem and you're testing a solution, you can go leaner."

---

## Content Structure

### Hook
Open with the interesting question or observation, not the framework.

**Example (DON'T):**
> "Let me tell you about prioritization frameworks."

**Example (DO):**
> "We had 47 things in the backlog. The PM said 'just pick the top 10.' That's not prioritization."

### Body
- Build the thinking
- Use a real example or story
- Show the pattern recognition from 15 years
- Reframe if needed

### Closing
- State the insight, not the action
- Let the reader think
- Avoid CTAs like "comment below for a free resource"

**Example (DO):**
> "The hardest product decisions aren't prioritization. They're identity decisions. Every roadmap is a statement about what the company refuses to become."

---

## What to Avoid

- ❌ Generic PM advice ("7 tips for...")
- ❌ Listicles without substance
- ❌ Prompts and ChatGPT regurgitation
- ❌ Buzzwords without explanation (synergy, leverage, optimize, disrupt)
- ❌ Trendy/casual tone (not your brand)
- ❌ Fake humility ("I'm just sharing what I've learned")
- ❌ Preachy tone ("You should...")
- ❌ Surface-level observations
- ❌ Emojis (unless genuinely part of a point)

## PM Problem-Solver Structure (CRITICAL)

**Rohit is a problem solver.** Every post follows this structure:

1. **Problem Statement** — Real, specific PM problem (not a trend)
   - Example: "You have 50 feature requests and no system to know which ones matter."
   - Not: "Prioritization is hard"

2. **PM Lens** — Why this problem exists (from PM perspective)
   - Example: "This happens because you treat all requests equally. You don't weight by impact and effort."
   - Shows PM thinking, not just observation

3. **PM Solution** — How a PM would actually solve it
   - Example: "Here's the scoring system I'd use: Impact (1-5) × Effort (-5 to 1) ÷ Risk. Plug them in. Your roadmap writes itself."
   - Actionable, rooted in PM thinking (discovery, data, prioritization, facilitation)

4. **Takeaway** — What to do differently tomorrow
   - Example: "Tomorrow: Pick your top 3 requests. Score them. You'll see immediately which ones matter."
   - Specific action, not vague advice

**Posts that FAIL:**
- ❌ "AI is changing discovery" (trend, no PM lens, no solution)
- ❌ "Prioritization is hard" (identifies problem, but no solution)
- ❌ "Great teams do X" (observation, no problem-solving)
- ❌ "Here's a framework" (no problem it solves)

**Posts that WIN:**
- ✅ "You have 50 requests and can't prioritize. Here's the exact scoring system I'd use." (problem + PM solution)
- ✅ "Your users aren't adopting the feature. Here's how I'd discover why in 2 days." (problem + PM approach)
- ✅ "Stakeholders disagree on roadmap. Here's how I'd align them around data." (problem + PM solution)

## Writing Checklist

Before publishing:

- [ ] Does this start with a REAL, RELATABLE problem (not a trend)?
- [ ] Is the problem humanly specific (not abstract)?
- [ ] Does it provide a clear, actionable solution?
- [ ] Can readers implement something tomorrow?
- [ ] Is the tone warm and direct, not preachy?
- [ ] Does it use real specifics, examples, or context?
- [ ] Would I want to read this from someone else?
- [ ] Does it map to one of the 5 pillars?
- [ ] Is there a hook that makes me want to read?
- [ ] **Does this deliver value or just identify a problem?** ← MOST IMPORTANT
