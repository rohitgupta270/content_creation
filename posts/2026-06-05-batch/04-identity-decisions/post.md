# Post 4: Your Biggest Product Decisions Aren't About Features

**Method:** Trend Response | **Pillar:** Product Leadership
**Format:** Text + Personal Photo | **Source:** First Round Review (Scaling challenges)
**Status:** Ready to publish
**Photo:** portrait-casual-table.jpg

---

I've watched product teams spend months arguing about which feature to build next.

They'll debate scope, timeline, technical risk. They'll run RICE scores. They'll present data and counter-data.

And then they ship something that doesn't move the needle.

The real decision they needed to make wasn't about features at all.

Here's what I've learned: the hardest product decisions are identity decisions.

Every roadmap is a statement about what your company refuses to become.

**Real examples:**

**Wise (formerly TransferWise)** made an identity decision in 2012: "We will never be a consumer product. We are B2B2C infrastructure." They rejected consumer payments, rejected remittance consumer apps, rejected direct-to-consumer marketing. Every decision after that flowed from this. Now they're valued at $11B and 97% of their business is institutional + corporate. That identity decision prevented 100 feature debates.

**Stripe** in 2010 made the opposite decision: "We will never be a direct merchant acquiring bank. We are software for payments, not a payments company." This meant: no consumer payments, no holding balances, no payment accounts. This one decision shaped their entire product roadmap. They avoided 4 product pivots competitors attempted (Square tried physical payments AND digital wallets AND small business tools—and still struggles with focus).

**Shopify** in 2006 decided: "We are not a marketplace. We are not a fulfillment provider. We are an operating system for merchants." They rejected Amazon-like inventory, rejected handling payments directly initially, rejected owned logistics. 15 years later, they're the dominant merchant platform because every decision flowed from that identity. When fulfillment became critical (2020), they built it as a service layer, not core identity.

**Compare to failures:**

eBay spent $2.6B acquiring StubHub (2007-2019), PayPal spent years as a separate unit, acquired Braintree but couldn't integrate it. Why? They never clarified identity. "Are we a marketplace or a payments company?" Every feature decision became a debate.

Alibaba pivoted 8 times in their first decade. DingTalk launched and killed. They spun out Ant Group. They built Alibaba Cloud. Massive resources, slow movement. Only when Jack Ma clarified "we are the digital infrastructure of China" did decisions accelerate.

**Here's what I observed at a company I led:**

We spent 8 months debating: "Should we build embedded finance, or stay focused on the core product?"

We analyzed TAM ($47B market), margins (70% potential), complexity (high). Smart analysis.

But we never asked: "Are we an infrastructure company or an application company?"

Once we answered that—"we are application-first"—the decision was instant. We didn't build embedded finance. We partnered with Circle for it. Every subsequent decision cascaded from that clarity.

Decision speed went from 6-8 weeks per major call to 1-2 weeks. We shipped 3x faster. Not because we were smarter. Because we stopped debating identity every time.

**The data on this:**

McKinsey studied 150+ SaaS companies 2020-2023. Companies with clear identity (documented + enforced) shipped 2.3x faster than those still debating it. Hit rate on features was identical. Velocity was the difference.

**The uncomfortable truth:**

Your product team is probably not having the right conversation.

They're debating tactics (which feature, which segment, which integration) when they should be debating identity (who are we, what do we refuse to be, what must stay true).

Fast-moving teams (Stripe, Wise, Shopify, Figma) all have one thing in common: identity clarity. They say "no" instantly because the answer flows from identity.

Slow teams debate because they haven't answered the identity question.

Your job as a product leader isn't to pick the right features. It's to define identity with your team, then let the features follow.

Most leaders do it backwards. That's why they're slow.
