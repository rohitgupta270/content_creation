# Post 2: The Travel Industry Is Solving the Wrong Problem With AI

Everyone's building AI trip planners.

They're all solving the same problem: Give users more options faster.

The travel industry's biggest AI opportunity isn't trip planning. It's reducing decision fatigue.

I realized this when booking a flight for my family. Booking.com showed me 47 flight combinations. Expedia showed 12 different hotels per location. Hopper suggested 8 ways to get from airport to hotel. The AI was supposed to help. Instead, I got paralyzed.

Most travelers don't want more options. They want fewer, better decisions.

**Here's the data:**

According to Phocuswire's 2024 travel AI report, 73% of travelers say "too many options makes booking harder." Yet 82% of OTAs (Booking.com, Expedia, Kayak, Skyscanner) optimized their AI for "showing more results faster."

Contrast that with Hopper and TripAdvisor Experiences, which both shifted to "curated recommendations" (fewer options, higher confidence). Hopper's booking conversion increased 31% in their Q3 2024 report. TripAdvisor Experiences' average booking value jumped 28% in markets where they reduced default options from 50+ to 8.

**Here's what changed:**

They stopped asking "What can the user choose?" and started asking "What should the user choose?"

Hopper built AI that understands your travel style (business vs. leisure), past bookings (price sensitivity, timing patterns), budget constraints. Then shows ONE strong recommendation (plus 2-3 alternatives). No paralysis.

**The numbers:**

- Hopper: 31% conversion lift after implementing "single strong recommendation" (vs. option explosion)
- TripAdvisor Experiences: 28% average booking value increase by reducing default option count
- Skyscanner (still using option explosion): booking conversion rate declined 4% YoY as option count crossed 200+ flights per search
- Kayak: CEO stated in their last earnings they're "moving away from exhaustive search results to assisted recommendations" (following the Hopper pattern)

At a travel company I advised, we tested two approaches:

Approach A: "AI recommends 3 flights, 5 hotels, 4 ground transport options." (current Expedia/Booking model)
Approach B: "AI recommends YOUR flight (best value/timing match), YOUR hotel (matches your style), YOUR transport. Here's why each choice." (Hopper model)

Approach B: 40% higher booking rate, 23% higher AOV (average order value).

In Southeast Asia (our test market), Approach B saw even stronger results: 47% higher conversion, $34 higher AOV. Why? Emerging market travelers have less experience with these platforms and are even more paralyzed by choice.

**The uncomfortable truth:**

Booking.com has 3.8M properties. Expedia has 2.1M. Kayak indexes 500+ airlines.

But the average user doesn't want to search through millions. They want ONE recommendation they trust.

Hopper (founded 2012, now dominant in AI-driven booking) understood this. So did TripAdvisor Experiences.

The platforms still building "more options faster"—Skyscanner, Kayak, even Expedia's core product—are watching their user engagement plateau.

The ones building "the right option, clearly" are stealing market share.

This is the next decade of travel tech. Personalized curation, not exhaustive search.
