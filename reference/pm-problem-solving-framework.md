# PM Problem-Solving Framework for Content

**Core Principle:** Rohit is a problem solver. Every post follows this framework.

---

## The PM Problem-Solving Structure

Every post has 4 parts:

### 1. Problem Statement
**What:** A real, specific problem PMs face (not trends, not observations)

**Characteristics:**
- Concrete and relatable (not abstract)
- Something the audience actually struggles with
- PM-relevant (affects product outcomes)
- Starts with a real scenario or pain point

**Examples of GOOD problem statements:**
- "You have 50 feature requests and no way to know which ones matter"
- "Your discovery process takes 6 weeks but you need answers in 2"
- "You're shipping features but users aren't adopting them"
- "Your payment flow has 40% abandonment and nobody knows why"
- "Your stakeholders disagree on what to prioritize"

**Examples of BAD problem statements:**
- "AI is changing product discovery" (trend, not a problem)
- "Teams struggle with prioritization" (too vague)
- "The future of payments is uncertain" (observation, not a PM problem)

---

### 2. PM Lens (Root Cause)
**What:** Why this problem exists, from a product manager's perspective

**This answers:**
- What assumption is wrong?
- What process is broken?
- What decision gap causes this?
- Why do so many PMs struggle with this?

**Characteristics:**
- Shows deep PM thinking
- Connects to discovery, data, prioritization, or stakeholder dynamics
- Not blaming the person — explaining the system
- Uses PM frameworks or patterns you've seen

**Examples:**

| Problem | PM Lens (Why It Happens) |
|---------|-------------------------|
| "50 feature requests, no way to prioritize" | "Teams treat all requests equally. They don't have a system to weight them by impact and effort. So everything feels equally urgent." |
| "Discovery takes 6 weeks" | "You're doing batch research instead of continuous discovery. You wait for perfect data instead of acting on signals as they come." |
| "Users aren't adopting" | "You solved for the feature, not for the user job. You never asked: What job are they trying to do? What are they using instead?" |
| "Payment abandonment is 40%" | "You blame UX. But you never actually discovered what causes abandonment. Is it trust? Complexity? Too many steps? Missing payment method?" |

---

### 3. PM Solution (How to Solve It)
**What:** A practical, actionable solution a PM would actually use

**Characteristics:**
- Rooted in PM thinking (discovery, data, prioritization, facilitation)
- Concrete and implementable (not theoretical)
- Uses frameworks or approaches that work
- Specific enough that readers can try it tomorrow
- May be a framework, process, or mindset shift

**Examples:**

| Problem | PM Solution |
|---------|------------|
| "50 feature requests, can't prioritize" | "Score each request: Impact (1-5) × Effort (-5 to 1) ÷ Risk. Plug them in. Your roadmap writes itself. Here's the actual formula..." |
| "Discovery takes 6 weeks" | "Stop doing batch research. Instead: 2 user interviews per week, continuous. Every Friday, synthesize what you learned. Adjust assumptions. This takes 20 hours/month, not 200." |
| "Users aren't adopting" | "Before building, answer: What job are they hiring this for? Run 5 problem interviews (not solution interviews). Here's how..." |
| "Payment abandonment 40%" | "Map the abandonment flow. At which step do users drop? (Trust? Complexity? Missing method?) Run 3 exit interviews. You'll know in 2 days what actually causes it." |

---

### 4. Takeaway (What's Different Tomorrow)
**What:** Concrete next action readers can take immediately

**Characteristics:**
- Specific action (not vague advice)
- Implementable tomorrow or this week
- Clear outcome ("you'll discover X" or "you'll save Y hours")
- Ties back to the original problem

**Examples:**

| Original Problem | Takeaway |
|-----------------|----------|
| "50 requests, can't prioritize" | "Tomorrow: Pick your top 3 requests. Score them using the Impact/Effort formula I shared. You'll see immediately which ones move the needle." |
| "6-week discovery" | "This week: Pick one assumption you're unsure about. Run 2 user interviews about it. You'll get signal in 3 days instead of 6 weeks." |
| "Users not adopting" | "Next Monday: Schedule 3 problem interviews with non-adopters. Ask: What job were you trying to do? What stopped you? You'll know the real blockers by Wednesday." |
| "Payment abandonment" | "Tomorrow: Follow 5 users through your payment flow. Note exactly where they drop. You'll have your root cause in 4 hours, not 6 weeks." |

---

## The Full Post Template

```
[HOOK: Real problem, relatable scenario]

[PROBLEM STATEMENT: Specific PM challenge]

[PM LENS: Why this happens, root cause analysis]

This is because [broken process / wrong assumption / missing data].

[PM SOLUTION: Practical, actionable approach]

Here's how I'd approach this:
1. [Step 1]
2. [Step 2]
3. [Step 3]

[Specific framework or example]

[TAKEAWAY: What to do tomorrow/this week]

Try this instead of that. You'll [discover X / save Y hours / get Z answer].
```

## Content Pillars as PM Problem-Solving

### Pillar 1: Future of Payments
Real PM problems:
- Discovery: "Users abandon at payment, why?"
- Prioritization: "Which payment integrations actually matter?"
- Tradeoffs: "Fraud prevention vs. frictionless experience"
- Strategy: "How do we compete in payments when competitors move 10x faster?"

### Pillar 2: Future of Travel
Real PM problems:
- Discovery: "Why do users abandon bookings?"
- Personalization: "How do I recommend the right trip for each user?"
- Complexity: "How do I simplify the booking flow without losing features?"
- Data: "How do I understand what customers actually value?"

### Pillar 3: AI × Product Management
Real PM problems:
- Discovery: "How do I use AI to understand users at scale?"
- Automation: "What should AI do vs. what should users do?"
- Strategy: "How do I design products that work WITH AI, not around it?"
- Organization: "How does my PM role change in an AI world?"

### Pillar 4: Product Leadership
Real PM problems:
- Alignment: "How do I get engineers and design to agree on direction?"
- Stakeholders: "How do I navigate conflicting leadership opinions?"
- Prioritization: "How do I say no to stakeholders with good ideas?"
- Tradeoffs: "How do I make hard calls when the data is ambiguous?"

### Pillar 5: Personal Life (10%)
Real problems:
- Balance: "How do I be a good parent and an ambitious PM?"
- Global work: "How do I navigate working across timezones and cultures?"
- Career: "How do I know when to move on or double down?"

## Quality Checklist for Every Post

Before publishing, verify:

- [ ] **Problem Statement** — Real, specific, PM-relevant (not a trend or observation)
- [ ] **PM Lens** — Explains WHY this problem exists from PM perspective
- [ ] **PM Solution** — Actionable, rooted in PM thinking (discovery/data/prioritization)
- [ ] **Takeaway** — Readers know what to do differently tomorrow
- [ ] **Relatable** — Uses real scenarios, examples, or stories (not abstract)
- [ ] **Practical** — Someone could actually implement this (not theoretical)
- [ ] **Problem-Solver Energy** — Feels like a PM helping other PMs, not a trend commentator
- [ ] **On-Pillar** — Maps to one of the 5 pillars
- [ ] **Voice** — Warm, witty, direct, evidence-based (Rohit's authentic voice)

**If ANY of these are missing, the post isn't ready.**
